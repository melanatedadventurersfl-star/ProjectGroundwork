# Groundwork Trust, Agreements & Conflict Resilience Framework v0.1

Status: Draft for review  
Linear: PRO-28, PRO-29, PRO-31 through PRO-42

## Purpose

Groundwork needs a reusable way to protect people, the company, person-to-company relationships, and person-to-person relationships. The framework reduces preventable ambiguity, identifies operational strain early, routes concerns fairly, preserves evidence, supports repair where appropriate, and enables clean separation when repair is unsafe or unsuccessful.

This is operating architecture, not universal legal advice. Contexts involving employment, housing, regulated services, safety, insurance, privacy, or significant financial exposure require appropriate professional review.

## Core principles

1. Clarity before reliance.
2. Written confirmation before material change.
3. Proportionate controls based on arrangement risk.
4. Facts, allegations, interpretations, findings, and decisions remain distinct.
5. Ordinary disagreement is not automatically misconduct.
6. Serious safety or legal concerns do not enter informal mediation by default.
7. Human review precedes consequential action.
8. Fairness does not eliminate accountability.
9. Company protection does not erase dignity.
10. Repair never requires forced reconciliation.
11. Exit is a designed process, not an improvised message.
12. Learning changes systems only when evidence justifies the change.

# 1. Protection model

Every agreement, policy, workflow, or decision should identify which interests it protects.

## 1.1 Individual person

Minimum protections include plain-language expectations, informed consent, privacy, dignity, access to relevant records, safe reporting, non-retaliation, proportional consequences, and a review path where appropriate.

## 1.2 Company or organization

Minimum protections include decision authority, payment and performance terms, confidentiality, property and access controls, accurate records, enforceable boundaries, fraud and misuse protections, escalation rights, insurance triggers, and continuity planning.

## 1.3 Person-to-company relationship

Minimum protections include shared purpose, role clarity, communication channels, response windows, change control, recalibration, neutral documentation, staged resolution, clean exit, and post-incident learning.

## 1.4 Person-to-person relationships

Minimum protections include consent, respect, boundaries, anti-harassment expectations, safe reporting, conflict-of-interest safeguards, power-imbalance review, facilitated repair for good-faith conflict, urgent escalation for serious safety concerns, and separation when continued interaction is unhealthy or unsafe.

# 2. Agreement architecture and role separation

## 2.1 Agreement types

Groundwork distinguishes at minimum:

- Community participation terms
- Volunteer agreements
- Employment agreements and policies
- Independent contractor agreements
- Vendor and supplier agreements
- Customer service agreements
- Partnership and collaboration agreements
- Leadership and governance commitments
- Property-use or occupancy agreements
- Confidentiality and data-access agreements

## 2.2 Role declarations

Every agreement identifies:

- Each party
- The role each party is acting in
- The organization or brand involved
- The decision owner
- Compensation or consideration
- Property, data, and system access
- Duration and review date
- Termination and transition rules

A person holding multiple roles receives separate role definitions. Friendship, family, romantic, community, housing, volunteer, contractor, worker, customer, and leadership roles must not silently merge.

## 2.3 Plain-language deal summary

Before signature or reliance, parties receive a short summary covering:

- What each person is agreeing to do
- What each receives
- What is paid in cash, credit, barter, reimbursement, or unpaid contribution
- When performance and payment occur
- What happens if work is incomplete
- How changes are approved
- How the arrangement ends
- Which document controls if messages conflict

## 2.4 Document hierarchy

Unless law or the agreement requires otherwise:

1. Signed controlling agreement
2. Signed amendment
3. Approved written change record
4. Official operational notice
5. Meeting recap acknowledged by relevant parties
6. Informal messages and conversations

Silence is not agreement. Temporary flexibility is not a permanent amendment.

# 3. Interaction classification and risk tiers

## 3.1 Classification dimensions

Each interaction is assessed using:

- Money or financial dependency
- Property or equipment access
- Physical safety exposure
- Authority or power imbalance
- Confidential or personal information
- Public or reputational impact
- Duration and dependency
- Regulation or licensing
- Reversibility of harm
- Multiple-role complexity

## 3.2 Risk tiers

### Tier 1: Low

Short duration, low money, low access, easily reversible. Requires basic expectations and contact ownership.

### Tier 2: Moderate

Repeated interaction, modest payment or access, meaningful member or customer impact. Requires written terms, assigned owner, change log, and exit process.

### Tier 3: High

Significant money, property, confidential data, power imbalance, long duration, safety exposure, or reputational impact. Requires formal agreement, professional review triggers, evidence plan, escalation owner, and periodic review.

### Tier 4: Critical

Regulated, legally sensitive, physically dangerous, high dependency, or potentially irreversible harm. Requires executive approval, specialized professional review, strict access controls, incident response, and documented go/no-go authority.

Risk reflects the arrangement and observed facts, not personality labels.

# 4. Assumptions, exceptions, and change control

## 4.1 Assumption log

Every material assumption records:

- Statement of assumption
- Owner
- Source
- Date recorded
- Validation method
- Review date
- Effect if false
- Current status

## 4.2 Exception record

Every exception records:

- Rule or term being varied
- Reason
- Scope
- Approver
- Start and expiration date
- Affected parties
- Risks and safeguards
- Renewal or rollback decision

Temporary exceptions expire automatically unless renewed in writing.

## 4.3 Material changes

Changes affecting payment, duties, access, property, timelines, safety, ownership, confidentiality, or termination require explicit confirmation. Verbal discussions are followed by a written recap identifying the proposed change and effective date.

# 5. Communication, notice, and acknowledgment

## 5.1 Communication classes

- Informal: social coordination and nonbinding discussion
- Operational: schedules, assignments, routine instructions
- Official: obligations, changes, approvals, performance concerns
- Urgent: time-sensitive safety or continuity matters
- Confidential: limited-access personal, personnel, legal, or commercial information
- Legally sensitive: notices, disputes, claims, investigations, or regulatory matters

## 5.2 State distinctions

- Sent: message transmitted
- Received: delivery reasonably confirmed
- Acknowledged: recipient confirms receipt
- Understood: recipient confirms their understanding
- Agreed: recipient affirmatively accepts the term or change

These states are not interchangeable.

## 5.3 Meeting recap standard

A material meeting recap includes decisions, owners, deadlines, unresolved questions, changes proposed, effective dates, and a correction window.

# 6. Evidence, records, and timeline integrity

## 6.1 Canonical records

Canonical records include signed agreements, amendments, invoices, ledgers, receipts, notices, approvals, role assignments, access logs, incident records, meeting recaps, and source communications.

## 6.2 Integrity rules

- Originals are preserved.
- Corrections append rather than silently overwrite.
- Version history remains available.
- Summaries link to source records.
- Material events receive timestamps and owners.
- Access is role-based.
- Evidence is preserved before removing accounts or access.

## 6.3 Fact separation

Records distinguish:

- Fact: directly supported event or record
- Allegation: claim not yet determined
- Interpretation: meaning assigned to facts
- Finding: conclusion after review
- Decision: authorized action taken

# 7. Early-warning model

## 7.1 Indicator groups

Observable indicators may include:

- Repeated missed commitments
- Unresolved payment discrepancies
- Multiple conflicting versions of terms
- Material work proceeding without approval
- Repeated ignored official communications
- Frequent exceptions without review
- Access or role use outside defined scope
- Increasing complaint frequency
- Recurring interpersonal breakdown affecting operations
- Records missing for high-risk decisions
- Repeated last-minute changes shifting cost or risk

## 7.2 Alert levels

- Observe: one low-confidence signal; collect context
- Review: repeated or material pattern; owner evaluates facts
- Intervene: confirmed operational risk; corrective plan required
- Escalate: urgent, high-impact, legal, safety, or repeated noncooperation concern

No alert is a permanent label. Human review is required before consequential action. Context, bias, power differences, and false positives must be considered.

# 8. Conflict classification and routing

## 8.1 Conflict types

- Misunderstanding or unclear expectation
- Performance or delivery issue
- Payment or agreement dispute
- Interpersonal conflict
- Policy or boundary violation
- Safety or urgent conduct concern
- Legal, regulatory, insurance, or public-impact matter

## 8.2 Routing

Each case receives:

- Classification
- Owner
- Response deadline
- Confidentiality level
- Required record
- Interim safeguards
- Resolution route
- Reclassification trigger

Ordinary disagreement may use clarification or facilitated conversation. Serious safety, legal, or regulatory matters bypass informal resolution and move to qualified review.

# 9. Staged conflict resolution

## Stage 1: Clarify

Confirm facts, controlling terms, perspectives, and immediate needs.

## Stage 2: Recalibrate

Restate roles, expectations, boundaries, and communication rules.

## Stage 3: Corrective or cure plan

Define action, owner, deadline, evidence of completion, and consequence of noncompletion.

## Stage 4: Facilitated conversation

Use only when appropriate, safe, and voluntary. The facilitator must be neutral enough for the matter.

## Stage 5: Formal review

Collect records, provide relevant parties an opportunity to respond, make findings, and issue a written determination.

## Stage 6: External escalation

Use qualified legal, HR, insurance, safety, financial, regulatory, or mediation support where required.

## Stage 7: Final action

Repair, monitoring, changed terms, suspension, separation, termination, restitution, or another authorized outcome.

Repeated noncooperation changes the available path. Resolution does not require reconciliation.

# 10. Confidential reporting, review, and non-retaliation

Minimum process:

1. Accessible reporting channel not controlled solely by the person involved
2. Intake acknowledgment and urgency assessment
3. Conflict-of-interest check for reviewers
4. Interim safeguards where needed
5. Limited-access fact gathering
6. Opportunity for relevant parties to provide information
7. Separation of allegations, evidence, findings, and decisions
8. Written outcome appropriate to each recipient
9. Review or appeal path where applicable
10. Non-retaliation monitoring

Privacy is protected, but absolute secrecy is not promised when safety, fairness, or law requires disclosure.

# 11. Separation, termination, and clean exit

Every exit process addresses:

- Voluntary or involuntary status
- Notice and effective date
- Cure opportunity or immediate-action basis
- Final payment, refund, reimbursement, or ledger reconciliation
- Return of property, equipment, credentials, and records
- Evidence preservation before access removal
- Confidentiality and continuing obligations
- Team or community communication on a need-to-know basis
- Future eligibility or contact boundaries
- Final decision record

# 12. Remedies, repair, and recovery

Available outcomes may include:

- Clarification or written correction
- Acknowledgment or apology
- Refund, repayment, replacement, restitution, or service correction
- Reperformance or corrective work
- Boundary, role, or access changes
- Training, coaching, monitoring, or probation
- Facilitated repair where all parties consent
- Separation and future-contact limits
- Community or reputation repair when public harm occurred

Remedies are proportional, owned, dated, tracked, and closed with evidence. A harmed person is never required to resume a relationship as proof of repair.

# 13. Post-incident learning

The after-action review asks:

- What happened?
- What was expected?
- Which facts are supported?
- What conditions contributed?
- Was the primary cause individual conduct, unclear terms, process failure, missing capability, weak supervision, or unavoidable uncertainty?
- What protected people and company effectively?
- What failed?
- Should Groundwork change an agreement, policy, training, staffing, insurance, tooling, or nothing?
- Could the proposed change create unfairness or excess burden?
- Who owns the corrective work and when is effectiveness reviewed?

Lessons are privacy-safe. One unusual event does not automatically create a universal rule. Repeated patterns justify stronger controls.

# Governance

The framework owner maintains version control, review cadence, professional-review triggers, emergency updates, and adoption evidence. Brand-specific policies inherit these structures but may add local legal, cultural, safety, and operational requirements.
