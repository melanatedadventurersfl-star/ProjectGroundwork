# Melanated Adventurers Minimum Valuable Experience v0.1

**Linear:** PRO-9  
**Status:** Proposed for review  
**Depends on:** MA Member Barrier Ranking v0.1

## Purpose

The Minimum Valuable Experience is the smallest coherent member journey that delivers the Melanated Adventurers promise. It is not a list of software features and it is not a reduced event calendar. It is the minimum end-to-end experience required for a curious person to move from uncertainty to participation, connection, and a believable next step.

## Product promise

A new member should be able to say:

> I understood what I was joining, chose an experience that fit me, arrived prepared, met people who noticed me, enjoyed the experience without feeling lost, and knew how to stay connected afterward.

## Primary product problem

An interested person may still lack the clarity, confidence, and human connection required to take the next step.

The first version must directly reduce four high-priority barriers:

1. Arriving without a human connection
2. Uncertainty about what to expect
3. Low confidence or beginner anxiety
4. Scattered communication

Cost, gear, and transportation remain important, but they are addressed through transparent options and operational support rather than becoming the center of the first product release.

# The Minimum Valuable Experience

## Phase 1: Understand

### Member need

Before committing, a person needs to understand what Melanated Adventurers is, who it serves, what the experience feels like, and whether beginners are welcome.

### Required experience

A prospective member encounters one clear MA introduction that communicates:

- MA is a Black-centered outdoor and adventure community.
- Experiences are intentionally curated, not anonymous public gatherings.
- Beginners are welcome when an experience is marked beginner-friendly.
- The member does not need to already own every piece of gear.
- The community values belonging, preparation, participation, and mutual care.

### Required capabilities

**Shared Groundwork capabilities**

- Community profile
- Story and media presentation
- Experience discovery
- Audience and suitability indicators

**MA-specific expression**

- MA mission and cultural identity
- Beginner-welcome language
- Real images and stories featuring Black and brown participants
- MA-specific participation expectations

### Barrier trace

- Representation and belonging
- Uncertainty about what to expect
- Beginner anxiety

---

## Phase 2: Choose

### Member need

The person needs to identify an experience that fits their interests, schedule, comfort level, budget, location, and preparation level.

### Required experience

Every published experience provides a consistent readiness snapshot:

- What the experience is
- Date, time, location, and duration
- Physical or skill expectations
- Beginner suitability
- What is included
- What the member must bring
- Gear, transportation, meal, or lodging options when available
- Total expected cost and important deadlines
- Accessibility and safety notes
- Who the experience is best suited for

The member can determine whether the experience is a good fit without searching through multiple platforms or message threads.

### Required capabilities

**Shared Groundwork capabilities**

- Structured experience profile
- Suitability and readiness attributes
- Included-versus-required information
- Pricing and deadline presentation
- Location and logistics information

**MA-specific expression**

- Outdoor difficulty language
- Gear guidance
- Camping, hiking, water, travel, or adventure-specific preparation
- MA package and add-on options

### Barrier trace

- Uncertainty about what to expect
- Lack of gear
- Transportation
- Cost
- Scattered communication

---

## Phase 3: Commit and Prepare

### Member need

After registering, the member needs one trusted preparation path rather than a trail of disconnected posts, emails, chats, and screenshots.

### Required experience

Registration creates a personal experience hub containing:

- Confirmation and payment status
- Final location and arrival instructions
- Packing or preparation checklist
- Schedule or itinerary
- Safety and weather information
- Contact method for questions
- Changes and announcements
- Gear, transportation, meal, and lodging selections
- A clearly identified host or welcome contact

Information may be delivered through email, text, or community channels, but the hub remains the canonical member-facing reference.

### Required capabilities

**Shared Groundwork capabilities**

- Registration record
- Personal itinerary
- Preparation checklist
- Announcement and change log
- Contact and support pathway
- Optional service selections

**MA-specific expression**

- Outdoor packing lists
- Weather-readiness guidance
- Tent, gear, transportation, meal, and lodging packages
- MA event policies and safety expectations

### Barrier trace

- Scattered communication
- Uncertainty about preparation
- Lack of gear
- Transportation
- Cost surprises

---

## Phase 4: Be Welcomed Before Arrival

### Member need

The member should not have to walk into the experience as an unknown person entering an already-formed circle.

### Required experience

Before the event, every first-time attendee receives at least one human connection:

- A welcome message from a host, ambassador, or designated member
- The name and recognizable photo of their welcome contact
- A simple introduction opportunity with one or more attendees
- A clear explanation of where to go and who to look for on arrival

For larger experiences, first-time attendees may be placed into a small welcome circle, crew, vehicle group, campsite cluster, or activity group.

### Required capabilities

**Shared Groundwork capabilities**

- First-time attendee identification
- Host or ambassador assignment
- Small-group assignment
- Introduction and contact permission

**MA-specific expression**

- MA welcome voice and norms
- Crew, campsite, vehicle, or activity-based grouping
- First-timer welcome ritual

### Barrier trace

- Not knowing anyone
- Fear of not belonging
- Beginner anxiety

---

## Phase 5: Arrive and Participate

### Member need

Arrival should confirm that the member belongs and knows what happens next.

### Required experience

The arrival experience includes:

- Visible and easy-to-understand check-in
- Recognition of first-time attendees
- Introduction to the welcome contact or small group
- Immediate orientation to the location, schedule, facilities, and safety information
- A low-pressure opening interaction that helps people learn names
- A clear way to request help without embarrassment

The member participates as part of a group rather than merely occupying the same venue.

### Required capabilities

**Shared Groundwork capabilities**

- Check-in
- Attendance and first-time status
- Host-facing arrival roster
- Group assignment display
- Support escalation

**MA-specific expression**

- MA arrival ritual
- Outdoor site orientation
- Safety briefing
- Group introductions and shared opening activity

### Barrier trace

- Not knowing anyone
- Beginner anxiety
- Representation and belonging
- Uncertainty at arrival

---

## Phase 6: Create a Meaningful Connection

### Member need

Attendance alone is not success. The member should leave having formed at least one genuine social connection or experienced a clear sense of being known.

### Required experience

During the experience, the operating design intentionally creates connection through at least one of the following:

- Small-group activity
- Shared meal
- Buddy or crew interaction
- Guided conversation
- Collaborative setup or service moment
- Reflection, storytelling, or closing circle

The experience should avoid forcing artificial networking. Connection is created through shared participation.

### Required capabilities

**Shared Groundwork capabilities**

- Small groups and roles
- Participation moments
- Story and reflection capture
- Recognition of contributions

**MA-specific expression**

- Campfire conversations
- Trail, float, campsite, travel, meal, or activity crews
- MA traditions and closing rituals

### Barrier trace

- Not knowing anyone
- Transactional attendance
- Weak belonging

---

## Phase 7: Continue After the Experience

### Member need

The experience should not disappear the moment everyone goes home.

### Required experience

Within a defined follow-up window, the member receives:

- A thank-you and recap
- Shared photos or stories when permission allows
- A simple feedback opportunity
- A way to reconnect with their group or welcome contact
- One recommended next experience
- A clear invitation to return, help, contribute, or learn more

The next step should reflect the member's stage. A first-timer is invited back. A returning member may be invited to help. A reliable contributor may see a leadership pathway.

### Required capabilities

**Shared Groundwork capabilities**

- Post-experience follow-up
- Feedback
- Story and media sharing
- Next-experience recommendation
- Journey stage and progression invitation

**MA-specific expression**

- MA recap voice
- Outdoor achievement and memory language
- Volunteer, ambassador, host, and trip-leader pathways

### Barrier trace

- Scattered communication
- No clear progression path
- Weak retention
- Loss of relationships after events

# Minimum journey summary

The first complete journey is:

1. **Understand** what MA is.
2. **Choose** an experience that fits.
3. **Prepare** from one trusted source.
4. **Meet someone** before arrival.
5. **Be welcomed** and oriented on arrival.
6. **Participate with people**, not anonymously beside them.
7. **Continue** into another experience or deeper role.

Removing any one of these stages creates a broken journey:

- Discovery without preparation produces uncertainty.
- Preparation without welcome leaves social anxiety untouched.
- Attendance without connection produces a transaction.
- Connection without follow-up allows the relationship to evaporate.

# Minimum capability set

## Must exist in the first product experience

1. Community introduction
2. Structured experience discovery
3. Readiness and suitability details
4. Registration and personal preparation hub
5. Canonical announcements and changes
6. First-time attendee identification
7. Welcome contact or small-group assignment
8. Host-facing check-in and arrival support
9. Intentional connection moment
10. Post-experience follow-up
11. Recommended next step
12. Basic feedback and journey measurement

These capabilities may initially be delivered through a combination of software and documented operating practices. The Minimum Valuable Experience is judged by the member outcome, not by whether every step is automated.

## Important but not required for the first release

- Full social feed
- Open direct messaging between all members
- Complex recommendation engine
- Gamified points or leaderboards
- Public follower counts
- Large achievement catalog
- Automated buddy matching based on advanced profiles
- Marketplace for third-party gear or travel
- Full volunteer workforce management
- Multi-brand identity switching
- AI-generated itineraries
- Advanced community health scoring

## Explicitly excluded

- Recreating Facebook, Meetup, Discord, or GroupMe inside Groundwork
- Treating content engagement as the primary measure of community health
- Launching every MA event type at once
- Requiring members to complete long profiles before participating
- Making members publicly disclose sensitive experience, accessibility, financial, or transportation needs
- Building features that do not trace to a ranked barrier or desired outcome

# Operational versus software responsibilities

The first release must not pretend software alone creates belonging.

## Software should make these reliable

- Clear information
- Consistent preparation
- First-time identification
- Welcome assignments
- Check-in visibility
- Follow-up timing
- Next-step invitations
- Measurement

## Human operations must deliver these

- Warmth
- Recognition
- Introductions
- Safety judgment
- Adaptation to individual needs
- Shared participation
- Cultural tone
- Trust

Groundwork supports the human experience. It does not replace it.

# Success measures

The Minimum Valuable Experience should be evaluated through member outcomes rather than vanity metrics.

## Activation

- Percentage of registered first-time attendees who complete preparation steps
- Percentage who can identify their arrival contact or group before the event
- Percentage who attend after registering

## Connection

- Percentage of first-time attendees who report meeting at least one person they would recognize or contact again
- Percentage who report feeling personally welcomed
- Percentage who participate in a small-group or shared activity

## Confidence and belonging

- Percentage who report knowing what to expect before arrival
- Percentage who report feeling prepared
- Percentage who report that MA felt designed with people like them in mind
- Percentage who would feel comfortable attending another MA experience

## Return and progression

- First-time attendee return rate within an appropriate experience window
- Percentage who accept a next-experience invitation
- Percentage who move from attendee to returning member, contributor, volunteer, ambassador, or leader
- Percentage of welcome assignments successfully completed by hosts or ambassadors

## Operational quality

- Number of critical questions answered outside the canonical experience hub
- Rate of missed or unclear arrival instructions
- Rate of unresolved gear, transportation, or accessibility needs
- Host-reported effort required to prepare and follow up with attendees

---

# Initial implementation principle

The first implementation should support one representative MA experience from discovery through follow-up. It should be complex enough to test preparation, welcome, participation, and continuation, but controlled enough to observe the member journey closely.

A successful first release is not the one with the most features. It is the one where a first-time attendee can honestly say:

> I knew what to do, someone was expecting me, I connected with people, and I want to come back.
