# Melanated Adventurers Member Barriers v0.1

**Status:** Proposed for review  
**Linear:** PRO-8  
**Purpose:** Rank the barriers that prevent people from discovering, joining, enjoying, returning to, and growing within Melanated Adventurers before features are proposed.

## Evidence standard

This first ranking is based on founder observations, event operations, repeated member questions, current workarounds, and known friction in the Melanated Adventurers journey. It is an informed operating hypothesis, not a substitute for interviews, surveys, attendance analysis, or controlled experiments.

Each barrier should be revisited as direct member evidence becomes available.

## Ranking method

Barriers are ranked using four factors:

1. **Reach:** How many prospective or current members may encounter it.
2. **Journey damage:** How likely it is to prevent the next meaningful step.
3. **Frequency:** How often it appears across experiences.
4. **Strategic fit:** How directly solving it supports belonging, confidence, participation, and leadership.

Severity uses four levels: **Critical, High, Medium, Low**.

---

## Ranked barriers

### 1. I do not know anyone, and I am unsure whether I will feel welcome

**Severity:** Critical  
**Journey stages:** Discovery, consideration, first attendance, early belonging

**Member perspective**

> I am interested, but arriving alone to a group where everyone may already know each other feels risky.

**Evidence and observations**

- Melanated Adventurers exists partly because representation and cultural belonging are missing in many conventional outdoor spaces.
- Newcomers frequently need reassurance about the group, the people attending, and whether the experience is beginner-friendly.
- Group identity may attract someone initially, but it does not automatically create a personal connection on arrival.

**Current workarounds**

- Founder-led welcomes and introductions.
- Group chats and social posts before events.
- Informal encouragement to bring a friend.
- Hosts personally connecting people during an event.

**Why it ranks first**

This barrier can stop the journey before the person attends. It also affects whether a first-time attendee converts into a returning member.

**Desired outcome**

A newcomer should be able to arrive alone and still feel expected, recognized, and connected to at least one person.

---

### 2. I do not know what to expect or how prepared I need to be

**Severity:** Critical  
**Journey stages:** Consideration, registration, preparation, arrival

**Member perspective**

> I cannot tell what the day will actually be like, what I need to bring, or whether I will be out of place.

**Evidence and observations**

- Outdoor and travel experiences often require detailed instructions, gear guidance, transportation information, meeting points, weather plans, and physical-exertion expectations.
- Event information is currently distributed across event pages, social posts, chats, emails, maps, and direct messages.
- Complex experiences create repeated logistical questions and last-minute uncertainty.

**Current workarounds**

- Eventbrite descriptions and FAQs.
- Social-media reminders.
- Group chats, maps, checklists, and direct answers from founders.
- Repeated announcements near the event date.

**Why it ranks second**

Uncertainty creates anxiety, support load, late cancellations, under-preparation, and poor arrival experiences. It is present across nearly every event type.

**Desired outcome**

A member should understand the experience, preparation requirements, risk level, schedule, location, and contingency plans without hunting across channels.

---

### 3. I am interested, but I do not have the confidence or experience to participate

**Severity:** High  
**Journey stages:** Discovery, consideration, preparation, participation

**Member perspective**

> Outdoor experiences look exciting, but I am not sure I am skilled, fit, experienced, or knowledgeable enough.

**Evidence and observations**

- Beginner access is central to the mission of reconnecting melanated people with outdoor experiences.
- Camping and adventure activities carry unfamiliar terminology, equipment, safety expectations, and social norms.
- Build-A-Camp and package-based support already compensate for part of this confidence gap.

**Current workarounds**

- Beginner-friendly messaging.
- Founder coaching and direct explanations.
- Equipment packages and prepared setups.
- Group-based experiences that reduce solo risk.

**Why it ranks third**

A welcoming community cannot create participation if people still believe the activity itself is beyond them.

**Desired outcome**

Members should see a clear beginner pathway and know which experiences match their comfort, ability, and readiness.

---

### 4. Information and communication are scattered

**Severity:** High  
**Journey stages:** Discovery through return

**Member perspective**

> I saw something on Facebook, but I do not know where the final details, updates, photos, or next event are located.

**Evidence and observations**

- Melanated Adventurers communicates across Facebook, Instagram, Meetup, YouTube, Discord, GroupMe, email, and event platforms.
- Changes to times, locations, weather plans, maps, packing instructions, and availability must often be repeated in several places.
- A member may follow one channel but miss the channel containing the operational update.

**Current workarounds**

- Manual cross-posting.
- Group chats and direct messages.
- Repeated reminders and screenshots.
- Founder memory acting as the informal integration layer.

**Why it ranks fourth**

This amplifies nearly every other barrier. It also creates heavy operational work and inconsistent member experiences.

**Desired outcome**

Every experience should have one authoritative member-facing home, with channel-specific communication pointing back to it.

---

### 5. Cost makes participation uncertain or inaccessible

**Severity:** High  
**Journey stages:** Consideration, registration, repeat participation

**Member perspective**

> I want to participate, but I cannot tell the full cost or whether there is an option that fits my budget.

**Evidence and observations**

- Experiences may combine admission, transportation, food, camping, rentals, lodging, equipment, and add-ons.
- The community has already used bundles, meal-only options, free participation options, shared transportation, and tiered packages.
- Price sensitivity has affected Build-A-Camp client decisions and is relevant to event participation.

**Current workarounds**

- Tiered ticket types.
- Bundles and group pricing.
- Bring-your-own options.
- Separating admission from optional services.

**Why it ranks fifth**

Cost is substantial, but it is not always the first barrier. Clear value, transparent totals, and flexible paths can reduce its impact.

**Desired outcome**

Members should understand the true total cost and have meaningful participation options at different price points.

---

### 6. I lack the gear or practical resources needed to join

**Severity:** High  
**Journey stages:** Consideration, preparation, participation

**Member perspective**

> I would go, but I do not own the equipment and do not know what is essential.

**Evidence and observations**

- The organization maintains a substantial inventory of tents, canopies, chairs, power equipment, cooking equipment, coolers, and other event resources.
- Build-A-Camp exists to reduce setup and equipment barriers.
- First-time campers may confuse optional comfort items with true requirements.

**Current workarounds**

- Rental and setup packages.
- Shared group equipment.
- Packing guidance.
- Founder-managed inventory and manual allocation.

**Why it ranks sixth**

This is a concrete barrier that the ecosystem is unusually positioned to solve, but it affects certain experience types more than the entire community journey.

**Desired outcome**

Members should know what they need, what they can borrow or rent, and what the organization already provides.

---

### 7. Transportation and distance prevent me from attending

**Severity:** Medium-High  
**Journey stages:** Consideration, registration, arrival

**Member perspective**

> I want to attend, but I cannot reliably reach the location or return safely afterward.

**Evidence and observations**

- Many experiences occur outside central Jacksonville or involve travel to Orlando, Brooksville, parks, campgrounds, and future destinations.
- The organization already plans group transportation for selected events.
- Remote outdoor locations may have limited cell service and complicated wayfinding.

**Current workarounds**

- Chartered or shared transportation.
- Meeting-point instructions.
- Maps and parking guidance.
- Informal ride coordination.

**Why it ranks seventh**

It can be a complete blocker for affected members, though it does not affect every local event equally.

**Desired outcome**

Members should be able to compare transportation choices, coordinate rides safely, and understand the full travel plan before committing.

---

### 8. I attended once, but I do not know how to become part of the community

**Severity:** High  
**Journey stages:** Post-event, return, contribution

**Member perspective**

> I enjoyed the event, but I do not know what happens next or how people become regular members, volunteers, or leaders.

**Evidence and observations**

- The company language distinguishes visitor, member, contributor, leader, steward, and legacy stages.
- Current events can create positive one-time experiences without a formal bridge to another gathering, crew, volunteer role, or leadership path.
- Founder capacity remains a bottleneck when progression depends on personal recognition and invitation.

**Current workarounds**

- Personal follow-up.
- Social posts about future events.
- Informal volunteer asks.
- Founder-selected leadership opportunities.

**Why it ranks eighth despite high severity**

It affects people after initial participation, so it does not block discovery or first attendance. However, it is one of the largest threats to durable community growth.

**Desired outcome**

Every member should be able to see a credible next step from attendee to returning member, contributor, and leader.

---

### 9. I cannot easily judge whether an experience fits my needs

**Severity:** Medium  
**Journey stages:** Discovery, consideration

**Member perspective**

> I cannot tell whether this experience is right for my age, mobility, comfort level, schedule, dietary needs, or preferred social environment.

**Evidence and observations**

- Experiences range from camping and floating to theme parks, social gatherings, travel, service, and educational programming.
- Accessibility, heat, physical exertion, food, lodging, bathroom access, and weather can materially change suitability.
- Questions are currently handled through descriptions and individual clarification.

**Current workarounds**

- Detailed event descriptions.
- Direct-message questions.
- Host judgment and manual recommendations.

**Why it ranks ninth**

It is important for trust and inclusion, but a stronger expectation-setting system can address much of it alongside barrier two.

**Desired outcome**

Members should quickly understand who an experience is designed for and what accommodations or limitations apply.

---

### 10. I do not see enough proof that people like me truly belong here

**Severity:** Medium  
**Journey stages:** Discovery, consideration, belonging

**Member perspective**

> The mission sounds right, but I need to see real people, real stories, and real experiences before I trust it.

**Evidence and observations**

- Representation is central to the brand.
- Event photos, member stories, milestones, and community history can reduce perceived social risk.
- Content is currently distributed across social platforms and is vulnerable to disappearing into feeds.

**Current workarounds**

- Social media imagery and event recaps.
- Group photos.
- Testimonials and word of mouth.

**Why it ranks tenth**

The existing brand already addresses representation better than conventional outdoor organizations. The remaining opportunity is to convert representation into durable social proof and living history.

**Desired outcome**

Prospective members should be able to see authentic stories, diverse participation, and evidence of long-term community care.

## Priority groups

### Immediate product focus

1. Arriving without a connection
2. Uncertainty about what to expect
3. Low confidence or beginner anxiety
4. Scattered communication

These barriers combine into a single strategic problem:

> A person may be interested in Melanated Adventurers but still lack the clarity, confidence, and human connection required to take the next step.

### Near-term operational focus

5. Cost clarity and participation options
6. Gear access
7. Transportation

These barriers require product support but also depend on operations, inventory, pricing, partnerships, and event design.

### Retention and community-growth focus

8. No visible progression path
9. Difficulty judging experience fit
10. Limited durable social proof

These become increasingly important after first-attendance conversion improves.

## Product implications without prescribing features

The ranking suggests that the Minimum Valuable Experience should first help a prospective or new member:

1. Understand the experience.
2. Determine whether it fits them.
3. Prepare with confidence.
4. Establish a human connection before or upon arrival.
5. See a clear next step afterward.

This document intentionally stops before selecting features. PRO-9 will convert the highest-ranked barriers into a Minimum Valuable Experience.

## Validation plan

The ranking should be tested through:

- Interviews with first-time attendees, returning members, volunteers, and people who expressed interest but did not attend.
- A short post-registration confidence survey.
- Cancellation and no-show reason capture.
- Support-question categorization by event.
- First-to-second-event conversion tracking.
- Volunteer and leadership progression tracking.

## Review questions

1. Are any major barriers missing?
2. Does the ranking reflect actual member behavior or primarily founder workload?
3. Which barriers affect specific experience types rather than the overall community?
4. What evidence would cause the top four ranking to change?
