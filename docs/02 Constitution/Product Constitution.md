# Product Constitution

## Project Groundwork

The Product Constitution defines the rules, principles, and decision-making standards that govern Groundwork.

The manifesto explains why Groundwork exists.

The constitution explains how Groundwork must behave.

This document should guide product decisions, feature prioritization, design choices, technical architecture, and future development.

---

# Article I: Purpose

Groundwork exists to help people create exceptional experiences with confidence.

The platform should reduce the effort, confusion, and anxiety involved in coordinating meaningful work.

Groundwork is not built to create more software activity.

It is built to make real-world work easier, clearer, and more repeatable.

---

# Article II: Organizing Principle

Groundwork organizes work around outcomes, not data types.

The primary user-facing concept is the Workspace.

A Workspace brings together the people, tasks, documents, resources, communications, timelines, and knowledge required to accomplish a specific purpose.

Users should not have to assemble the story of their work from disconnected modules.

The work itself should have a home.

---

# Article III: Context Before Content

No object should exist without context.

Every task, document, message, file, budget item, inventory item, note, decision, incident, and lesson should belong somewhere meaningful.

Context makes information useful.

Without context, information becomes clutter.

Groundwork should always make it clear:

- What this is
- Where it belongs
- Why it matters
- Who is responsible
- What happens next

---

# Article IV: Action Over Information

Groundwork should not overwhelm users with raw information.

The platform should translate information into clear action.

Every screen should help answer:

> What should I do next?

If a screen does not help a user understand, decide, act, or learn, it should be reconsidered.

---

# Article V: Calm Over Clutter

Groundwork should reduce organizational anxiety.

The interface should feel calm, organized, and capable.

The platform should surface what matters, hide what does not, and make the next step obvious.

Complexity may exist in the system, but it should not be pushed onto the user all at once.

---

# Article VI: One Source of Truth

Groundwork should avoid duplicated data.

The same fact should not need to be entered in multiple places.

If information is reused, it should be referenced, not copied.

If duplicated data becomes necessary, the system should clearly identify which source is authoritative.

Duplicate data without ownership is a product failure.

---

# Article VII: History Matters

Every meaningful object should have a history.

Groundwork should preserve:

- When something was created
- Who created it
- When it changed
- Who changed it
- Where it was used
- What decisions were made
- What happened afterward

History turns activity into knowledge.

Knowledge turns repetition into improvement.

---

# Article VIII: Knowledge Compounds

Every completed Workspace should make future Workspaces smarter.

Groundwork should preserve lessons learned, templates, reusable checklists, budget history, vendor notes, venue feedback, staffing patterns, inventory usage, and operational improvements.

Work should not disappear when it is completed.

It should become reusable intelligence.

---

# Article IX: Automation Before Repetition

If a user repeats a process often enough, Groundwork should eventually help automate it.

Automation does not mean removing humans from the process.

It means removing unnecessary manual friction.

Examples include:

- Reusing previous timelines
- Generating packing lists
- Creating tasks from templates
- Flagging missing requirements
- Suggesting next actions
- Detecting risks
- Reminding owners before deadlines

Automation should support judgment, not replace it.

---

# Article X: Role-Aware Experience

Groundwork should adapt to the user's role.

A volunteer, team lead, organizer, chapter leader, worker, client, and administrator should not see the same experience.

Each person should see what is relevant to their responsibility.

The platform should answer four questions for each user:

1. What am I responsible for?
2. What needs my attention now?
3. What happens next?
4. Who do I need to work with?

---

# Article XI: Capabilities Over Hardcoded Modules

Groundwork should be built from reusable capabilities.

Capabilities are tools that can be enabled within Workspaces.

Examples include:

- Tasks
- Timeline
- Budget
- Documents
- Inventory
- Communications
- Forms
- Ticketing
- Knowledge Base
- Analytics
- AI Assistance

Capabilities should be designed once and reused across many Workspace types.

This prevents Groundwork from becoming a pile of one-off features.

---

# Article XII: Systems Remain Beneath the Surface

Systems are an important architectural layer, but they should not dominate the user experience.

Internally, Groundwork may organize information through systems such as Experience, Inventory, Volunteer, Marketing, Finance, Fleet, and Knowledge.

Externally, users should primarily interact through Workspaces and actions.

Users should not need to understand the internal architecture to get work done.

---

# Article XIII: Design for Expansion Without Losing Simplicity

Groundwork should support growth without becoming bloated.

The platform should be able to serve:

- A small community group
- A growing service business
- A regional chapter network
- A nonprofit
- A festival team
- A brewery
- A volunteer organization
- A future enterprise customer

However, flexibility must not come at the cost of clarity.

A powerful system that feels confusing has failed its users.

---

# Article XIV: Templates Are Institutional Memory

Templates are not shortcuts.

Templates are preserved knowledge.

A template should capture the useful structure of previous work, including timelines, checklists, budgets, roles, resources, risks, communications, and lessons learned.

Groundwork should make it easy to turn completed work into reusable systems.

---

# Article XV: AI Must Be Grounded

AI inside Groundwork should act as an operational advisor, not a novelty.

It should use the organization's actual data, history, templates, timelines, and current state to provide practical guidance.

AI should help users:

- Identify risks
- Understand priorities
- Draft plans
- Summarize progress
- Compare current work to past work
- Recommend next steps
- Preserve lessons learned

AI should avoid unsupported claims and should clearly distinguish between known information, assumptions, and suggestions.

---

# Article XVI: Progress Is a Product Feature

Groundwork should make progress visible.

Users should be able to understand the health, readiness, and momentum of a Workspace without digging through every detail.

Progress indicators should be meaningful, not decorative.

Examples include:

- Workspace Health
- Readiness Score
- Timeline Status
- Budget Variance
- Inventory Preparedness
- Crew Coverage
- Risk Level
- Open Blockers

Progress should show where attention is needed.

---

# Article XVII: Every Feature Must Justify Its Existence

Every feature must answer three questions:

1. Why does this exist?
2. What problem does it eliminate?
3. Which Groundwork principle does it support?

If a feature cannot answer these questions, it does not belong yet.

Ideas may be saved.

Not every idea should become a feature.

---

# Article XVIII: Build for Real Work

Groundwork should be designed around how people actually coordinate work in the real world.

Real work is messy.

People forget things.

Volunteers arrive late.

Weather changes.

Budgets shift.

Equipment breaks.

Venues have strange rules.

Messages get lost.

Groundwork should not assume perfect conditions.

It should help people navigate imperfect conditions with more confidence.

---

# Article XIX: The Platform Should Explain Itself

Users should not need a manual for common actions.

The product should use clear language, sensible defaults, contextual prompts, examples, and guided flows.

When a user is blocked, Groundwork should explain what is missing and how to move forward.

The system should teach good operations by how it is designed.

---

# Article XX: The Standard of Success

Groundwork succeeds when users feel more prepared, more organized, and more confident.

Success is not measured only by usage.

It is measured by whether Groundwork helps people:

- Plan better
- Prepare earlier
- Execute with less confusion
- Preserve what they learned
- Repeat success
- Avoid preventable mistakes
- Grow without losing control

Groundwork should make first-time organizers feel experienced and experienced organizers feel unstoppable.

---

# Closing Principle

Groundwork should never become software for software's sake.

It exists to support the people who make things happen.

Every screen, capability, object, automation, and decision should serve that purpose.

If Groundwork stays faithful to this constitution, it can grow without losing itself.
