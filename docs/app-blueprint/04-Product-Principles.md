# 04. Product Principles

## Document Status

- **Version:** 1.0
- **Milestone:** 1 — Product Foundation
- **Status:** Approved working principles

## Purpose

These principles guide product, design, content, engineering, and operational decisions. They are intended to resolve tradeoffs when multiple solutions appear reasonable.

## 1. Experience Before Features

A feature should exist because it improves the member journey, not because another product includes it.

Before building, define:

- the member problem;
- the desired real-world outcome;
- the smallest useful solution;
- how success will be measured.

## 2. Community Before Content

People may first arrive for an adventure, but they stay because they feel connected.

Content should help members prepare, learn, connect, remember, or act. The platform should not reward empty volume.

## 3. Outdoors Before Screens

The app is a bridge, not the destination.

During an active adventure, interfaces should be glanceable, calm, and practical. Avoid unnecessary prompts, animations, or engagement requests that interrupt the moment.

## 4. Beginners Deserve Clarity, Not Condescension

Explain expectations, skill levels, gear, weather, transportation, accessibility, and safety in plain language.

Never assume knowledge. Never make inexperience feel embarrassing.

## 5. Progress Should Be Earned in Real Life

Passport stamps, Trail Marks, ranks, and milestones should reflect meaningful participation, learning, service, or contribution.

Do not reward compulsive checking, empty posting, or artificial streaks.

## 6. Belonging Must Be Designed

Welcoming language alone is not enough.

Belonging requires:

- representative photography;
- clear expectations;
- transparent pricing;
- accessible preparation;
- visible safety practices;
- culturally aware moderation;
- thoughtful introductions and group dynamics.

## 7. The Next Step Should Always Be Clear

At every major stage, the member should know what to do next.

Examples:

- discover an adventure;
- review fit and requirements;
- register;
- prepare;
- check in;
- capture a memory;
- choose what comes next.

## 8. Personalization Without Chaos

The Metro-inspired tile dashboard may eventually support rearranging, resizing, and pinning. Personalization should increase relevance without making the interface difficult to understand or support.

Default layouts must remain excellent.

## 9. Useful Motion, Not Decorative Noise

Live tiles and transitions should communicate change, status, or priority.

Motion must remain subtle, accessible, and optional where appropriate. Important information cannot depend solely on animation.

## 10. Safety Is a Product Feature

Safety must be present across discovery, registration, preparation, check-in, community interaction, moderation, and incident response.

The platform should make it easy to understand:

- difficulty and physical expectations;
- weather and environmental conditions;
- required equipment;
- meeting locations;
- emergency contacts;
- organizer identity;
- reporting options.

## 11. Privacy Should Be the Default, Not a Patch

Members must understand what is public, shared with participants, visible to organizers, or private.

Location, attendance history, photos, family information, health-related needs, and contact information require special care.

## 12. Accessibility Expands Adventure

Accessibility is not limited to technical compliance. It includes physical access, sensory needs, mobility, communication, financial clarity, transportation, and experience-level access.

The product should state limitations honestly rather than hiding them.

## 13. Trust Requires Accuracy

Dates, times, prices, addresses, capacity, inclusions, requirements, and updates must be treated as operational data, not decorative content.

When information changes, affected members should see what changed and when.

## 14. Design for the Field

Members may use the app in bright sunlight, rain, low signal, crowded check-in areas, or while carrying gear.

Important screens should support:

- high contrast;
- large touch targets;
- quick scanning;
- offline or low-connectivity resilience where feasible;
- minimal typing;
- clear confirmation states.

## 15. One Ecosystem, Distinct Communities

Melanated Adventurers, MANA, Build-A-Camp, and future initiatives may share infrastructure, but they should not be collapsed into one indistinct experience.

Shared systems should preserve each community's audience, purpose, voice, and permissions.

## 16. Build the Smallest Complete Journey

A release should support an end-to-end member outcome rather than many disconnected screens.

For example, it is better to fully support discovering, registering for, preparing for, and completing one adventure than to launch shallow versions of ten unrelated modules.

## 17. Human Operations Remain Visible

The platform should not pretend that every concern can be automated. Members need clear access to organizers, support, policies, and human judgment when situations are complex.

## 18. Memory Over Metrics

The Passport and Journey should feel meaningful, not clinical. Statistics support the story, but they should not replace it.

The emotional center is “what happened and who was there,” not merely totals.

## Principle Conflict Resolution

When principles conflict, prioritize in this order:

1. safety and dignity;
2. trust and accuracy;
3. accessibility and clarity;
4. successful real-world participation;
5. community connection;
6. product distinctiveness;
7. growth and monetization.

## Current Decision

These principles apply to all future product requirements and should be referenced in design reviews and decision records.

## Open Questions

- Which accessibility standards and testing process will be required before launch?
- What low-connectivity capabilities are essential for the first release?
- How much dashboard personalization should be included after the default experience is validated?
- Which safety information is organizer-entered versus system-required?
