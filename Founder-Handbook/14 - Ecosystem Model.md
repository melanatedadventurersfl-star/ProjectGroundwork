# Ecosystem Model

**Version:** 0.1  
**Status:** Conceptual architecture

The company builds distinct, healthy communities supported by shared knowledge, methodology, and infrastructure.

## Structural Layers

### Company Foundation

The internal source of direction and standards:

- The Compass
- Company Genome
- Community methodology
- Product methodology
- Naming and decision frameworks
- Experience standards
- Pattern library

### Groundwork

The shared foundation that provides reusable Building Blocks, Capabilities, and Services.

Groundwork should remain neutral about camping, beer, networking, and other brand-specific contexts. Those meanings belong to the brands.

### Public Brands

Each brand serves a distinct primary community and must remain complete on its own:

- Melanated Adventurers
- Build-A-Camp
- Beerded Empire
- JaxBlack
- Future brands that pass the Compass Filter

### Curated Experiences

Experiences are where the brand promise becomes tangible. They include physical, digital, and hybrid interactions, but should favor meaningful human connection over passive screen time.

## Ecosystem Principle

The ecosystem should enhance each brand without flattening their differences.

Brands may share:

- infrastructure;
- operational knowledge;
- proven patterns;
- analytics methods;
- technical capabilities.

Brands should not be forced to share:

- audience identity;
- public voice;
- culture;
- mission;
- customer-facing accounts or rewards without a clear benefit and consent.

## Ecosystem Test

A new initiative should be evaluated with four questions:

1. Does it strengthen its own community?
2. Does it offer a useful benefit to the broader ecosystem?
3. Could it weaken, confuse, or compete with another brand?
4. Does it teach the company something reusable?

The first question is required. The second and fourth are advantages. A harmful answer to the third requires redesign.

## Shared Wisdom

The company should share more than code.

When one brand discovers a repeatable pattern, the lesson should be captured and made available to other brands.

Examples:

- welcoming first-time participants;
- introducing newcomers to existing members;
- designing beginner pathways;
- developing volunteers into leaders;
- closing an experience with reflection and continuation;
- reducing operational friction before an experience begins.

## Pattern Library

Reusable learning should be organized into a pattern library.

Initial categories:

- Community patterns
- Experience patterns
- Leadership patterns
- Operational patterns
- Technology patterns
- Communication patterns

Patterns are recommendations grounded in evidence and experience, not rigid rules applied without context.

## Conceptual Flow

```text
Company Foundation
        ↓
Groundwork
        ↓
Distinct Public Brands
        ↓
Curated Experiences
        ↓
Connection, Belonging, Growth, and Transformation
        ↓
Reusable Learning Returns to the Company
```

This learning loop is the primary compounding advantage of the ecosystem.
