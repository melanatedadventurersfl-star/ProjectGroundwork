# Melanated Adventurers Newcomer Orientation v0.1

Status: Draft for review  
Linear: PRO-11  
Parent: MA Minimum Valuable Experience v0.1

## Purpose

The newcomer orientation helps an interested person quickly understand what Melanated Adventurers is, decide whether a specific experience fits them, and take the next step without needing outdoor expertise, insider knowledge, or an existing social connection.

The orientation is not a corporate onboarding course. It is a warm, compact confidence bridge.

## Orientation promise

By the end, a newcomer should be able to say:

> I understand what MA is, I can see myself here, and I know which next step makes sense for me.

## Primary barriers addressed

1. Uncertainty about whether MA is for them
2. Not knowing what to expect
3. Beginner anxiety and low outdoor confidence
4. Fear of arriving alone or not belonging
5. Scattered or jargon-heavy information

## Entry points

The orientation must work when entered from:

- An event page
- Social media
- A referral from a member
- A QR code at an in-person event
- The MA home page
- A follow-up message after someone expresses interest

Every entry point should lead to the same canonical orientation content.

## Orientation structure

### 1. What is Melanated Adventurers?

Core message:

Melanated Adventurers creates welcoming, curated outdoor and social experiences centered on people of color. You do not need to be an expert, own special gear, or already know someone to participate.

Required content:

- Community purpose
- Who the experiences are designed for
- What “curated” means
- Clear statement that beginners are welcome
- Clear statement that people may attend alone

### 2. What does participating feel like?

Explain the experience in human terms rather than event-production language.

A newcomer should understand that MA experiences generally include:

- Clear preparation guidance
- Hosts or welcome contacts
- Intentional introductions and shared activities
- Space to participate without performing expertise
- A community-centered rather than anonymous event atmosphere

### 3. Which experience is right for me?

Every experience should communicate suitability through the same five signals:

1. Activity type
2. Physical intensity
3. Experience level
4. Social format
5. Preparation burden

Suggested plain-language scales:

- **Experience level:** First-timer friendly / Some familiarity helpful / Experienced participants
- **Physical intensity:** Easygoing / Moderate / Challenging
- **Social format:** Highly social / Balanced / Independent-friendly
- **Preparation:** Show up ready / Bring a short list / More planning required
- **Commitment:** A few hours / Full day / Overnight or multi-day

No outdoor jargon may be required to interpret these signals.

### 4. What will I need?

The orientation should distinguish between:

- What MA provides
- What the participant must bring
- What may be rented or added
- What is optional
- What support is available

The content must normalize asking questions and not owning gear.

### 5. What happens after I choose?

The newcomer should see the immediate next steps:

1. Open the experience brief
2. Register or express interest
3. Receive preparation details from one trusted source
4. Meet or be introduced to a welcome contact
5. Arrive knowing where to go and who expects them

## Recommended first-touch copy

### Headline

**You do not have to be an outdoor expert to start.**

### Supporting message

Melanated Adventurers creates welcoming experiences where people of color can explore, connect, and build community. Come with experience, curiosity, or simply the desire to try something new.

### Reassurance points

- Beginners are welcome
- Coming alone is normal
- Preparation guidance is provided
- Many experiences include gear or support options
- A real person will help you know what to expect

### Primary action

**Find an experience that fits me**

### Secondary action

**See how a first experience works**

## First-experience explainer

The orientation should include a brief walkthrough:

### Before

You choose an experience, review one preparation guide, and receive a welcome contact.

### When you arrive

You check in, receive a quick orientation, and are introduced to at least one person or small group.

### During

You participate at the level described in the experience brief. Questions and beginner needs are expected, not treated as interruptions.

### After

You receive a follow-up, the next relevant experience, and an easy way to stay connected or contribute.

## Content rules

The newcomer orientation must:

- Use plain language
- Lead with belonging and confidence, not logistics
- Avoid assuming prior outdoor experience
- Avoid generic claims such as “something for everyone”
- State audience and boundaries honestly
- Make solo attendance feel normal
- Identify what varies by experience
- Link to one canonical experience brief
- Work on a mobile screen
- Be understandable in under five minutes

## What this orientation is not

It is not:

- A full history of the organization
- A founder biography
- A long FAQ
- A membership application
- A gear catalog
- A public social feed
- A personality quiz
- A complicated recommendation engine
- A replacement for the experience-specific brief

## Shared Groundwork capabilities

These parts should eventually be reusable across brands:

- Audience and promise presentation
- Experience suitability signals
- Beginner-readiness indicators
- Preparation expectation summaries
- Canonical next-step routing
- Entry-source tracking

## MA-owned expression

These remain specifically owned by Melanated Adventurers:

- POC-centered purpose
- Community voice and tone
- Outdoor and adventure context
- Cultural cues and representation
- Definitions of beginner-friendliness
- Experience imagery and storytelling

## Human operating responsibilities

Software and content cannot create belonging by themselves. MA operators remain responsible for:

- Answering newcomer questions
- Making honest suitability judgments
- Assigning welcome contacts
- Escalating accessibility or preparation concerns
- Ensuring the real experience matches the orientation promise

## Validation checklist

Before release, test the orientation with people who:

- Have never attended an MA event
- Do not regularly participate in outdoor activities
- Plan to attend alone
- Have concerns about cost or gear
- Enter from different channels

Ask each person:

1. What is MA?
2. Do you believe this is for someone like you?
3. Which experience would you choose and why?
4. What would you need to do next?
5. What still makes you hesitant?

## Success measures

Initial measures:

- Orientation completion or meaningful engagement
- Percentage selecting an experience afterward
- Reduction in repeated basic preparation questions
- Self-reported clarity about what to expect
- Self-reported confidence in taking the next step
- Percentage of solo newcomers who proceed to registration

## Definition of done

PRO-11 is complete when:

- The orientation message and content structure are approved
- Suitability signals are standardized
- First-touch and first-experience copy are ready for design
- The orientation links cleanly to the canonical experience brief
- The content has been tested with at least three newcomer perspectives
- Unresolved findings are recorded rather than hidden
